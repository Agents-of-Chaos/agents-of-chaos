01 the problem

Concealed indirect injection

LLM-based agents increasingly process external data such as emails, documents, and code repositories. This exposes them to indirect prompt injection, where adversarial instructions embedded in that content redirect agent behavior without the user's awareness.

The paper highlights concealment as an underexplored dimension. Because users typically see only the agent's final response, an attack can succeed while presenting no clue of compromise, leaving harmful outcomes to be accepted as legitimate.

02 the setup

A public competition across three settings

The authors ran a large-scale public red-teaming competition evaluating this dual objective across three agent settings: tool calling, coding, and computer use.

The scale was substantial: 464 participants, 272,000 attack attempts, 13 frontier models, and 41 scenarios, yielding 8,648 successful attacks.

03 the findings

Universal weaknesses

All models proved vulnerable, with attack success rates spanning 0.5% to 8.5%. The authors identify universal attack strategies that transfer across 21 of 41 behaviors and across model families, which they read as evidence of fundamental weaknesses in instruction-following architectures.

Capability and robustness showed weak correlation. Gemini 2.5 Pro exhibited both high capability and high vulnerability, indicating that stronger agents are not automatically safer ones.

the bottom lineEvery frontier agent tested could be hijacked by hidden instructions in external content, and the most transferable attacks point to structural weaknesses in how these models follow instructions.
