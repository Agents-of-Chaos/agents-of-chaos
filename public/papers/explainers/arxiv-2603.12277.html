01 the problem

Roles are labels, not perceptions

An LLM receives a single text stream partitioned into roles such as system, user, or tool. The authors argue that models judge the source of text from how it sounds rather than from its labeled role.

A command hidden in a webpage can hijack an agent because it sounds like trusted text, despite carrying an untrusted label. The paper frames this as role confusion, the root cause behind prompt injection.

02 the method

Measuring who the model thinks is speaking

The authors design role probes to measure how an LLM internally perceives who is speaking. Using these probes, they find that injected text lands in the same representational space as the trusted role it imitates.

This gives a concrete internal signal for injection: role confusion becomes a measurable property of the model's representations rather than an abstract failure mode.

03 the attack

CoT Forgery and its predictability

CoT Forgery is a zero-shot attack that injects fabricated reasoning into user prompts and tool outputs. Models mistake the forged reasoning for their own thoughts, producing 60% attack success against frontier models against near-zero baselines.

The degree of role confusion predicts attack success before any token is generated, and the mechanism extends to standard agent prompt injections.

04 why it matters

Implications for agents

For multi-agent and tool-using systems, trust boundaries rest on role labels that models do not enforce internally. If sounding like a role is indistinguishable from being one, injected content in tool outputs or retrieved pages can impersonate trusted sources.

The role probe offers a pre-generation signal that could be used to detect confusion, which is directly relevant to red-teaming and monitoring agent pipelines.
